\subsection{Spectral Analysis}

\subsubsection{Purpose and signal preparation}
The aim of the spectral analysis is to identify disturbances that are not atmospheric in origin, such as parachute oscillations, payload rotations, and high-frequency sensor noise. Since the sensors are mounted on small probes descending under parachutes, part of the measured variability can be produced by the motion of the CanSat itself rather than by changes in temperature, pressure, particulate matter, CO\textsubscript{2}, or wind. The spectral workflow is therefore used as a diagnostic step before the atmospheric interpretation: it allows us to locate suspicious periodic components, remove them when justified, and retain a signal that is more representative of the real vertical structure of the atmosphere.

The workflow is applied to the usable high-quality datasets selected in the data quality assessment, with particular focus on VAMOS and ScamSat. Since the mechanical artefacts of interest are tied to parachute flight, the spectra are computed only on the detected descent intervals. For VAMOS, the descent window is identified from the pressure record as the interval between apogee and landing, from 3313.8 to 3513.2 s, corresponding to 199.5 s and a peak altitude of about 681 m AGL. For ScamSat, the descent is identified independently from the altitude record as the interval from maximum altitude to the first return below 30 m AGL, from 636 to 990 s, corresponding to 354 s and a peak altitude of about 679 m AGL. Restricting the analysis to these windows avoids mixing aircraft vibration before release or ground-level sensor noise after landing with the descent dynamics.

For each variable, invalid warm-up values are removed where necessary and the samples are placed on a uniform time grid. This is necessary because FFT-based methods assume a constant sampling interval; otherwise, irregular timestamps would be incorrectly interpreted as changes in frequency content.

\subsubsection{Low-pass filtering}
A low-pass filter is used to produce the smoothed atmospheric profiles shown later in the report. The physical atmospheric signal is expected to vary slowly compared with the mechanical motion of the payload: pressure changes mainly because the probe descends, while temperature, CO\textsubscript{2}, and particulate matter evolve over comparatively long time scales. For this reason, a zero-phase Butterworth low-pass filter is used to smooth the raw profiles without shifting features in time or altitude. The cut-off frequency is chosen below the Nyquist frequency of each dataset and high enough to preserve the broad atmospheric trend. This filtered copy is not used to search for spectral peaks, because a low cut-off could suppress the pendulum band that the analysis is designed to test.

\subsubsection{Trend removal}
After uniform resampling, the dominant descent trend is removed when the goal is to study the residual signal in the frequency domain. This step is especially important for pressure and temperature, because the descent produces a large low-frequency component that can hide weaker periodic disturbances. A linear trend is subtracted from the uniformly sampled signal, and the remaining residual is treated as the approximately stationary time series used for spectral analysis. The residual is not interpreted as the final atmospheric profile; it is used only to detect periodic artefacts and sensor noise.

\subsubsection{FFT, Welch PSD, and spectrograms}
The detrended residuals are analysed with three complementary methods. A Hann-windowed FFT is used to inspect the amplitude of individual frequency components, while Welch's power spectral density (PSD) is used to estimate how the signal energy is distributed across frequency in a more stable way. Welch's method divides the signal into shorter overlapping windows, computes the spectrum of each window, and averages the result; this reduces the sensitivity to isolated transients and makes recurring oscillations easier to identify. A short-time Fourier transform (STFT) spectrogram is then used to check whether any candidate peak is persistent throughout descent or appears only as a transient event.

The expected pendulum band is taken as 0.3--1.0 Hz, corresponding to suspension lengths of roughly 0.3--3 m. VAMOS pressure, temperature, and wind are sampled at approximately 1 Hz, so only the lower part of this band, about 0.3--0.5 Hz, can be resolved without aliasing. ScamSat acceleration is sampled at about 28 Hz during descent and therefore provides the most complete mechanical check of the full expected pendulum band.

\subsubsection{Spectral results and interpretation}
The descent-only spectra do not show a robust narrowband parachute resonance. The strongest peaks in the expected pendulum band are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:spectral_descent_peaks}. In VAMOS pressure and wind, the peak-to-background ratios are below unity, indicating that the nominal peak is weaker than the local spectral background. VAMOS temperature shows a slightly larger ratio, but the peak is close to the Nyquist limit and is not supported by the other VAMOS channels. ScamSat acceleration, which is the best-resolved mechanical diagnostic, has its strongest peak near 0.328 Hz, but the peak-to-background ratio is only about 1.17 and the spectrogram does not show a persistent horizontal band. The signal is therefore better interpreted as weak broadband mechanical variability rather than a sustained pendulum mode.

\subsubsection{Identification and removal of external disturbances}
The final step is to decide whether a frequency component can be attributed to an external disturbance. A component is considered suspicious only if it is narrowband, physically plausible, and persistent across the relevant diagnostics within the descent window. In the time-frequency domain, such a disturbance should appear as a stable horizontal band in the spectrogram, whereas atmospheric variability is expected to be slower, broader, or more transient.

The only coherent low-frequency feature is found in the VAMOS wind-direction yaw proxy: during descent, the unwrapped wind bearing has a weak component near 0.021 Hz, corresponding to a period of about 48 s, with a net rotation of only about -0.10 turns. This is too slow to be interpreted as a fast pendulum or spin resonance and more likely represents gradual payload yaw, wind-direction shear, or a combination of both. Since no stable mechanical resonance is identified, no notch filter is imposed. The atmospheric profiles are therefore taken from the low-pass-filtered signal, avoiding the removal of physically meaningful variability.


\subsection{Data Analysis and Visualization}
\noindent Once the cleaned atmospheric signals are obtained, the processed variables are visualised to extract physically meaningful information about the vertical structure of the atmosphere during the descent. Temperature, pressure, CO\textsubscript{2}, and particulate matter (PM\textsubscript{2.5} and PM\textsubscript{10}) are plotted as vertical profiles, allowing direct inspection of how each quantity varies with altitude and enabling comparison with the expected signatures of the temperature inversion described above. Wind speed and direction are visualised using a compass rose, showing the distribution of wind directions and their associated magnitudes, and a hodograph, a graph in which the east--west and north--south components of the wind vector are plotted against each other at successive altitude levels \citep{lohmann2024}. This representation reveals how wind speed and direction evolve with height, providing insight into the vertical wind shear structure of the boundary layer during the descent.
